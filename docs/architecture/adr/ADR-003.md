# ADR-003: Represent piano as weighted undirected graph

**Status**: Accepted

**Context**
The piano side of the problem must encode which note pairs are important to preserve relative to each other. Early attempts used binary adjacency (chromatic neighbours only) or equally weighted edges for a handful of intervals (fifth, third, octave). These approaches either ignored perceptually relevant gradations or imposed arbitrary weights.

**Decision**
Create an undirected weighted graph **Gₚ = (Vₚ, Eₚ, W)** where:
- Vₚ = set of piano notes (MIDI numbers 0‑107 for a full 88‑key instrument, or a subset for the MVP).
- For each unordered pair {i,j} (i≠j) compute the weight  
  \[
  W_{ij} = \frac{1}{D(r_{ij})},
  \]
  where D is the sensory dissonance from the Plomp‑Levelt model using the equal‑tempered frequency ratio \(r_{ij}=2^{\Delta_{ij}/12}\) and Δ_ij is the pitch‑class distance in semitones (the shorter way around the chromatic circle).  Set W_ii = 0.
- Optionally normalise the entire matrix by its maximum value to keep numbers bounded for the optimiser (this scaling does not affect the arg min).

This construction yields a **principled, parameter‑free** similarity matrix grounded in psychoacoustics: the more consonant the interval, the larger the weight; the more dissonant, the smaller (approaching zero for highly dissonant pairs such as minor seconds or tritones).

**Alternatives Considered**
1. **Hand‑designed interval weights** (e.g., chromatic=1, fifth=0.8, third=0.6, octave=0.7) – introduces arbitrary constants that lack empirical justification and poorly capture the fine gradations of consonance across the chromatic scale.
2. **Binary adjacency for chromatic neighbours only** – disregards the importance of harmonic intervals (fifths, thirds, octaves) that are known to shape fingering and hand motion.
3. **Weight based on empirical co‑occurrence in a music corpus** – would make W dependent on a specific repertoire, violating the goal of a corpus‑free, theory‑driven model.
4. **Use of the tonnetz or other harmonic graph structures with uniform edge weights** – fails to differentiate consonance vs. dissonance (e.g., treats a perfect fifth and a tritone equally if both are edges in the graph).

**Consequences**
- **Positive**: W now reflects real psychoacoustic similarity; the optimiser is incentivised to keep consonant note pairs close in movement‑cost space, directly addressing the desire to preserve musical structure without embedding arbitrary numbers.
- **Negative**: Requires computation of the Plomp‑Levelt dissonance sum (over a limited number of harmonics, e.g., K=L=8) for each of the N(N‑1)/2 pairs. With N ≤ 88 this is trivial (< 1 ms). Memory usage is O(N²) (~ 7 KB for 88 notes).
- **Impact on Objective**: The final QAP objective becomes  
  \[
  \min_{f} \sum_{i,j} \frac{1}{D(r_{ij})} \cdot C_{f(i),f(j)} .
  \]
  All constants in D (ERB parameters, harmonic amplitude envelope) are derived from literature or physiological measurement; no free musical weights remain.
